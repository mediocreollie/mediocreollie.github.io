I recently joked in a job interview that there were so many mistakes to pick from when asked what I’d learned from one.

The interviewer asked what I had learned from a mistake and I replied, “I hope not”, which I found strange afterwards.

I’d say I make mistakes every day. Usually small ones, like missing an obvious checkmate in chess or getting off a climb too early. Sometimes bigger ones, like having awful meals because I experimented too much cooking, or adding an extra 20 minutes to a journey trying to save 5.

So why does the word “mistake” have such a stigma around it? People talk about mistakes like they should be rare, when in reality they are part of doing anything interesting.

The reason I make mistakes is because I’m trying new things, experimenting with different approaches and learning from them. If you never make mistakes, you probably aren’t pushing yourself very far outside what you already know.

Obviously there’s a difference between reckless mistakes and thoughtful ones. Repeating the same mistake over and over is different to trying something, failing, adjusting and improving.

Most people admire the results of experimentation, but forget the mess that came before it. Every skill, invention, business, recipe or achievement is built on a pile of failed attempts nobody talks about anymore.

If humans never made mistakes, would we even have half the world we live in now?
