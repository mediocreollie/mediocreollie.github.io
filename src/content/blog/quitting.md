“The quitter”

The quitter is not looked at positively.

Giving up on something, especially if you’re good at it, is often received with disappointment and claims of regret. Yet we celebrate people who quit smoking or drinking because the negative impacts of those are obvious.

Like many things in life, it is easier to support someone else quitting than it is to support ourselves. Whether it is a toxic job, relationship or habit, it is easier to spot the signs from the outside than it is when you’re living through it yourself.

People love the phrase “never quit”, but I think that only really works if the thing you are holding onto is still making your life better. Persistence is valuable, but blind persistence can trap people in situations they’ve already outgrown.

Sometimes quitting is not failure, it is just change.

I’ve quit a number of hobbies over the years, such as the gym, gaming and boxing. At the time it felt strange because those things became part of my identity, and walking away from them almost felt like losing progress. But realistically, interests change as people change.

There is also a weird social pressure around sticking things out purely because of the time already invested. People stay in jobs, degrees, friendships and routines because they’ve already spent years on them, even when they know they are unhappy.

But if quitting one thing opens the door to something healthier, more fulfilling or more aligned with who you are now, was it really quitting in the negative sense people mean?

Maybe the better question is: what is the difference between quitting and changing direction, and why are they treated so differently?
